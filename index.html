Data Transformation & Design Chocies

We state our data transformations here for ease of readability for our captions. We assume the data to be missing almost completely at random, so we use methods of mean imputation and selective removal to maintain key trends without introducing bias to the data. We try to avoid removal of data points completely so as to not erase potential trends or introduce bias. We also use some linear regression to infer missing values for variables such as lot size and pricing. We also selectively scaled the y-axis to make the visualization clearer. We also grouped flip_ind (binary flipping or not) to be aggregated into flip_count, so that we can see the number of properties flipped. We also used price_dif_pch (percent change in price), as it acts as a more accurate representation of profit from flipping. Also, instead of having 4 separate columns buyer_bnk_ind, buyer_bus_ind, buyer_gov_ind, buyer_gse_ind, buyer_llc_ind to each have a binary 0 or 1 based on what kind of buyer, I transformed/grouped these 4 columns into “buyer” with categorical variables of bnk, bus, gov, gse, llc. Also, for the last figure (figure 10), I used a log scale for the color gradient to be able to pick up on the more sensitive trends in our data, which worked well to conserve key relationships.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

The analysis presented provides a multi-faceted exploration of the housing market in Greater Boston, shedding light on the key factors and issues driving high property prices, the geographic influences on pricing and property flipping, and the role of various investors in shaping market dynamics (i.e. our overall analysis questions). Through our study of price distributions by building style, trends over time, and property-specific pricing, we observe that residential properties tend to have the highest average prices, with notable spikes in conventional housing around 2014. This implies market volatility and potential economic and policy influences on pricing fluctuations. Our geographical analysis reveals distinct trends in pricing and property flipping across different areas, particularly in high-value zones like the North End, where commercial properties exhibit significantly higher sale prices than residential buildings. The spatial distribution of house flipping indicates that investors are more active in specific regions, and properties with a longer flip horizon tend to command higher prices, hinting at speculative market behavior where investors hold onto properties to maximize future gains. This aligns with the broader issue of housing speculation, as properties are increasingly treated as financial assets. Investor characterization further deepens our understanding of Boston’s housing crisis, revealing that while non-investors dominate transactions, institutional investors consistently achieve the highest price percent differences on their flips, securing the most profitable returns. In contrast, smaller investors frequently experience losses, indicating market inefficiencies and competitive disadvantages that may drive smaller players out of the market. The investor purchase-sale relationships further illustrate a concerning market, where larger investors often buy from one another, suggesting a closed system where the rich become richer at the expense of Boston residents. My insights suggest that Boston’s affordability crisis is fueled by a combination of high demand, limited supply, speculative investment, and an investor hierarchy that continues to profit and drive Boston housing affordability down for the rest of us. Addressing these challenges requires policy interventions, such as extensions to the Affordable Homes Act, that prevent speculation, incentivize affordable housing development, and promote access to housing across different income levels. Further research, such as for the final project, could go into the specific policies that could mitigate these issues to create a more balanced, sustainable, and healthy housing market in Greater Boston.
